## Part 1: Friction Metrics

### What is a Friction Metric?

Friction refers to anything that slows a user down, confuses them, or prevents them from completing what they set out to do. A **friction metric** is a measurable signal that quantifies that difficulty.

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Cognitive** | Too much to think |
| **Interaction** | Hard to do |
| **Emotional** | Feels wrong or risky |

### When / Why / How to Use

- **When:** Maps measurement to the full product lifecycle — discovery/baseline → design/prototyping → post-launch → ongoing
- **Why:** Turns opinion-based debates into evidence. Covers the business, design, and user case.
- **How:**
  1. Define the flow and the goal
  2. Choose your metrics
  3. Set up measurement
  4. Analyse and identify friction points

### Friction Analysis: Current Experience

| User Step | Friction Observed | Friction Type | Severity | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sign up / Onboarding | Too many fields to fill in at once | Cognitive | High | Users abandon before completing |
| Finding a feature | Navigation is unclear, buried 3 levels deep | Interaction | Medium | Lowers task completion rate |
| Submitting a form | No confirmation feedback after submission | Emotional | High | User feels uncertain — trust is lost |
| Error recovery | Error message is vague, no guidance to fix | Cognitive | Medium | Increases frustration & support tickets |

### Friction Analysis: Proposed Stage Experience

| User Step | Friction Reduced / Eliminated | Design Solution | Expected Outcome | Priority |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sign up / Onboarding | Cognitive overload from long form | Progressive disclosure — break into steps | Higher completion rate | P1 — Critical |
| Finding a feature | Unclear navigation structure | Flatten IA + add search / shortcut | Faster task completion | P2 — High |
| Submitting a form | No feedback after submit | Success state + confirmation message | Builds trust, reduces anxiety | P1 — Critical |
| Error recovery | Vague error message, no fix guidance | Inline error + actionable hint text | Fewer drop-offs, better recovery | P2 — High |

## Part 2: Assumptions

### What is an Assumption?

> Anything you believe about your user, problem, or product **without evidence** to confirm it.

| Type | Example |
|---|---|
| **User Assumption** | Our users are 25–35 and tech savvy |
| **Problem Assumption** | This problem is real and urgent |
| **Solution Assumption** | A star rating will build trust |
| **Business Assumption** | Users will pay $99/month |

> **Assumptions aren't bad. Hidden assumptions are.**

### How to Write a Strong Assumption

Formula:
> We believe that **[WHO]** will **[WHAT]** because **[WHY]**.  
> We'll know this is true when **[SIGNAL]**.

| Weak | Strong |
|---|---|
| Users will like the app | We believe freelance designers will log time daily because they struggle to invoice accurately. We'll know this when 60% log a session in week 1. |
| People hate slow loading | We believe mobile users will abandon checkout if load time exceeds 4 sec. We'll know this when abandonment rate correlates with load time in analytics. |

### The Assumption Map

|  | **Weak Evidence** | **Strong Evidence** |
|---|---|---|
| **More Important** | 🔴 Test first — high importance, low confidence, validate before you design | 🟡 Validate early — high importance, high confidence, still worth checking |
| **Less Important** | ⚪ Monitor later — low importance, low confidence, not worth research spending now | 🟢 Safe to proceed — low importance, high confidence, move forward |

### When to Run an Assumption Map

1. Before you design anything
2. Before committing to a big feature
3. When the team is disagreeing
4. After every round of user research
5. Before pitching to stakeholders

### Practice 1 — Create Assumptions _(10 mins)_

Write down everything you currently believe about your user. No filtering.

**How to do it:**
- Use AI to write 5 assumptions about your product using the formula
- Embed resource to AI for training
- Use prompt to do

**Debrief (5 min):**

## Part 3: Research Questions

### What is a Research Question?

| What | How | When |
|---|---|---|
| A focused question that guides what you need to learn from users — rooted in an assumption you want to validate or disprove | Starts with the assumption — then asks what you still need to learn to prove or disprove it. Not a survey question or yes/no — it's open-ended and exploratory. | After you've mapped your assumptions and identified which ones are most risky or unknown. Before planning your interview guide — it becomes the anchor for every interview question you write. |

**Write it as:**
> "What do we need to understand about [user behavior / problem / context] in order to [make this design decision]?"

**Rule:** One assumption = one research question. Revisit if your assumption changes.

### From Assumption to Interview Guide

```
1 assumption → 1 primary research question → 5–8 interview questions
```

### Why Research Questions Matter

1. Prevent scope creep
2. Force clarity on purpose
3. Connect to an assumption
4. Focus synthesis

### Good vs Bad Research Questions

| Weak | Strong |
|---|---|
| Do users like our product? | What moment in onboarding causes users to disengage, and what do they believe at that moment? |
| What features do users want? | What workarounds are users using for [task], and what do those reveal about unmet needs? |
| Why don't users trust us? | What signals do users look for before submitting payment? |

## Part 4: User Interview

### Interview Fundamentals

| Principle | Guidance |
|---|---|
| **Ask open questions** | "How do you currently do this?" — not "Do you like this?" |
| **Follow the story** | When something sounds interesting, dig: "Tell me more about that." |
| **Stay curious, not leading** | Never confirm assumptions. Let their answer surprise you. |
| **Listen more than you talk** | Silence is OK. Wait. Let them fill the space first. |
| **Observe, don't just hear** | What they do ≠ what they say they do. Note both. |

> 💡 **Golden rule:** Understand their world — don't validate your solution.

### How AI Helps at This Stage

AI is your research thinking partner — not a replacement for real conversations.

| 🧠 Generate & pressure-test assumptions | ❓ Co-write interview questions | 📋 Synthesize messy notes | 🔍 Challenge your thinking |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dump what you think you know. AI flags stereotypes and gaps you didn't notice. | Give AI your user type + topic. Review and pick the best open-ended questions. | Paste raw interview notes. AI maps observations to Empathy Map quadrants. | Ask: "What would prove this assumption wrong?" — builds real research rigour. |
