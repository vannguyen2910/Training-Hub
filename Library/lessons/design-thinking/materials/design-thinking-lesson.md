## Overview

Most UX designers don't struggle because they lack design skills. They struggle because they skip the thinking that makes design decisions defensible. This session introduces Design Thinking — not as an abstract methodology, but as a practical process that changes how you start, run, and close design work.

The session opens by surfacing the real problems designers face: jumping to high-fidelity design too quickly, taking briefs as truth without questioning them, and working in isolation from the people the product is for. These aren't skill problems. They're process problems.

Design Thinking is introduced as a human-centered, problem-solving method that helps teams understand user needs before designing solutions. The 5-stage framework — Empathise, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test — is taught as a flexible cycle, not a rigid checklist. Students learn that most teams start at Ideate, skipping the two stages that actually shape whether the final design solves the right problem.

By the end of the session, students have applied the framework to their own real project, mapping out which activities they will run at each stage and what outputs they will produce.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, students will be able to:

1. **Explain** what Design Thinking is and why it exists, in language they could use with a product manager or stakeholder
2. **Identify** the 5 stages of the Design Thinking cycle and what question each stage is trying to answer
3. **Recognise** where their current design process typically breaks down — and why that stage matters
4. **Select** appropriate activities for each stage of the framework applied to their own project
5. **Draft** a project-specific design process map that names the activities and outputs for each stage
6. **Apply** the framework to a real brief, stakeholder context, or existing project before the next session

## Core Content

### The Real Problem: Design Without Thinking

Most UX designers spend 90% of their time in the bottom layers of the product designer capability pyramid — tools, visual design, UX design. These are important. But the areas that actually shape career growth — design process, communication, collaboration, ownership — don't get enough space.

More critically, designers often work in a pattern that feels productive but produces bad outcomes: a requirement arrives, they open Figma, they produce high-fidelity screens. Nobody questioned the brief. Nobody validated whether the problem was real. Nobody checked who else should be in the room. Six months later, the product ships and users don't respond the way anyone expected.

This is not a skills problem. It is a process problem — specifically, the absence of a structured approach for understanding the problem before designing the solution.

**Without a design process:** solutions are decided before problems are understood, briefs are treated as truth, assumptions are invisible until it's too late, and late pivots are expensive.

**With a design process:** decisions are grounded in user evidence, assumptions are surfaced early, research actually changes what gets built, and there are fewer surprises at launch.

---

### What is Design Thinking?

Design Thinking is a human-centered problem-solving method that helps teams understand user needs, challenge assumptions, and create innovative solutions.

The key word is *human-centered*. It doesn't start with a business requirement or a technical constraint. It starts with understanding the people the product is for — their context, their actual behaviour, their unspoken needs — before any solution is proposed.

Design Thinking exists because most teams default to solution-first thinking. They receive a brief, accept it as a correct description of the problem, and move straight into building. Design Thinking interrupts that pattern by insisting that understanding must come before designing.

---

### The Product Designer Capability Pyramid

The Artiom Dashinsky pyramid describes what product designers are actually evaluated on, from base to apex:

- **Base:** Tools, Visual Design, UX Design — the skills most design hiring tests
- **Middle:** Collaboration, Communication, Design Process — the skills that determine day-to-day effectiveness
- **Top:** Ownership, Strategy, Mentorship, Advocacy — the skills that drive career growth and business impact

90% of professional development discussions focus on the base. But the designers who move into senior and lead roles are the ones who invest in the middle and top layers — particularly *design process* and *communication*.

Design Thinking is the methodological foundation for the Design Process layer. It doesn't replace craft. It's the decision-making structure that makes craft decisions worth making.

---

### The 5-Stage Cycle

The 5-Stage Design Thinking cycle consists of:

**01 — EMPATHISE**
*What is actually going on with users — not what we assume?*
Understand the people you're designing for deeply: their feelings, motivations, behaviours, and pain points. This is done before any solution thinking begins.

**02 — DEFINE**
*What is the real problem worth solving?*
Using what you learned in Empathise, articulate a clear, specific problem statement that the team can align on and design against. A brief is not a problem definition — the Define stage creates one.

**03 — IDEATE**
*What are all the possible ways to solve it?*
Quantity before quality. Generate as many ideas as possible before evaluating them. Most teams skip Empathise and Define, and start here — which means they're generating solutions to a problem they've never validated.

**04 — PROTOTYPE**
*What is the fastest way to make this testable?*
Build the minimum thing that allows you to test your ideas with real users. Wireframes, interactive prototypes, proof-of-concept builds. Not a finished product — an argument you can test.

**05 — TEST**
*Were we right? What do we need to change?*
Show the prototype to real users and observe what happens. Synthesise findings. Return to earlier stages if the learning demands it.

**Important:** Design Thinking is non-linear. You move freely between stages as insights emerge. Completing a test might send you back to Define. A Define session might reveal you need more Empathise work. The stages are a scaffold, not a fixed sequence.

---

### The Pattern Most Teams Follow

In practice, most teams skip stages 1 and 2 entirely — not because they don't know them, but because nobody asked for them.

The brief arrives from a stakeholder or product manager, treated as if it already contains the problem definition. The team moves straight to Ideate: brainstorming solutions to a problem they've accepted without questioning. Sometimes they prototype. Sometimes they test. Sometimes testing is cut when time is short.

Empathise and Define aren't skipped because designers don't care about users. They're skipped because the organisational process doesn't create space for them. Nobody scheduled a user research session. Nobody built an assumption audit into the kickoff. Nobody wrote a research brief before the design sprint started.

*"A brief is not a problem definition."*

This session creates the vocabulary and tools for students to advocate for that space.

---

### What Good Process Looks Like

A good Design Thinking process, applied to a real project, produces these outputs at each stage:

1. **Understand users first (Empathise)** — Research Brief, user interviews, assumption map
2. **Frame the real problem (Define)** — Insight statements, problem statement, JTBD mapping
3. **Organise for users (Ideate)** — Evidence-based sitemap, task flows, concept sketches
4. **Build to answer questions (Prototype)** — Wireframes, interactive prototype, decision log
5. **Find out if you were right (Test)** — Test synthesis, iterated prototype

---

### Stage 1 — Empathise

The Empathise stage is about deeply understanding the people you're designing for — not just their actions, but their feelings, motivations, and pain points. Four modes of empathic research: **Observe, Interview, Immerse, Engage.**

**Discovery Activities** (understand users before any solution thinking begins):

- **Desk research** — Competitor review, market reports, analytics, existing research. Done before primary research to avoid re-learning what's already known.
- **User survey** — A quantitative snapshot. Good for scale, limited for understanding. Use to supplement interviews, not replace them.
- **User interviews** — Open questions, probing techniques, avoiding leading language. The richest source of qualitative insight.
- **Shadowing / observation** — Observe without interfering. Surface workarounds and habits users don't think to mention.
- **Stakeholder interviews** — Understand business context, constraints, and assumptions. Not a substitute for user research.
- **Support ticket / NPS analysis** — What are users already saying? Mine for patterns before primary research.

**Synthesis Activities** (turn raw observations into a clear picture):

- **Empathy mapping** — What users say, think, do, and feel. Forces distinction between observation and inference. Quadrants: SAY / THINK / DO / FEEL.
- **Assumption Map** — Surface everything the team believes without evidence. Move entries from Unknown to Confirmed or Unconfirmed.
- **Research Log update** — Add an entry after every user conversation: quote, behaviour, context. Builds an ongoing evidence base.

---

### Stage 2 — Define

The Define stage takes raw research from Empathise and turns it into a clear, validated problem statement the whole team can design against.

**Key tools:**

- **Lean UX Canvas** — Frames the business problem, the user, the solution hypothesis, the metrics, and the assumptions in one view.
- **Jobs-to-Be-Done (JTBD) mapping** — Identifies functional (task), emotional (feeling), and social (perception) jobs. Most designs miss the emotional and social jobs, which is often where the real value is.
- **How Might We (HMW)** — Converts each insight into a design opportunity question. Not too broad (unsolvable) and not too narrow (already a solution in disguise).
- **Current-state journey mapping** — Maps the experience as it actually is, not as it should be. Reveals friction, workarounds, and emotional lows.

---

### Stage 3 — Ideate

The Ideate stage is about generating as many possible solutions as possible before committing to one. Quantity first, quality later.

**Generate activities:**
- **Crazy 8s** — 8 sketches in 8 minutes. Forces volume, kills perfectionism. No filtering during generation.
- **HMW ideation** — Take each HMW question and generate 5–10 ideas against it. The question is the constraint that makes ideas useful.
- **Brainwriting** — Silent, parallel idea generation in writing. Prevents groupthink and loudest-voice bias.
- **Ideate Workshop** — Run Crazy 8s + HMW Ideation back-to-back in a 30-min sprint. Together they produce 50+ ideas to dot-vote from.

**Select activities:**
- **Impact vs effort matrix** — Plot ideas by impact (user value) vs effort (build cost). Focus on the top-left quadrant.
- **Concept sketching** — Develop the top 2–3 directions into fuller sketches with a narrative.
- **Storyboarding** — Narrative of how the concept plays out for a user in their real context.
- **Proposed process map** — Map the experience as it should feel after the solution is built.

---

### Stage 4 — Prototype

The Prototype stage is about making ideas testable as quickly as possible. Not building a product — building an argument.

**Activities:**
- **Wireframes** — Structural layout showing content hierarchy and interaction patterns. No colour, no brand, no distraction.
- **Interactive prototype** — States, transitions, navigation demos. Cover key interaction states: default, loading, empty, error, success.
- **Proof of Concept (POC)** — Answers one question: does this idea actually work? Keep it rough and fast.

---

### Stage 5 — Test

The Test stage finds out whether the design solves the problem — and changes what doesn't, based on evidence.

**Activities:**
- **Internal feedback session** — Structured critique using "I noticed / I wonder / What if" — not "I like / I don't like." Documents decisions before shipping.
- **Concept testing** — Show a concept (not a working prototype) to 3 users. Measure comprehension: do they understand what it is and why it would be useful?
- **Moderated / Unmoderated usability testing** — Facilitator observes a user completing real tasks live. Think-aloud protocol. 5 users reveals approximately 85% of critical issues.
- **A/B testing** — Compare two live versions with real traffic. Answers "which is better?" not "why?" Use after qualitative research has shaped the variants.

## Activities

### 🗣️ Opening Activity — One Struggle
**Type:** In-class · Verbal share
**Time:** 10 min
**Format:** Full group

What's one moment you struggled when starting a new initiative?

1. Take 2 minutes to think of a real project moment — not a hypothetical. What made it hard?
2. Share one sentence with the group: describe the project briefly and what the struggle was.
3. Listen for patterns as others share — unclear brief, no user access, jumped straight to design, nobody agreed on the problem.

This activity establishes that the session's content is about real, shared experiences — not abstract theory.

---

### 🗺️ Activity — Draft Your Design Process
**Type:** In-class · FigJam, Miro, or Notion
**Time:** 10 min
**Format:** Solo, using your real project

Apply the 5-stage Design Thinking framework to your own project.

**Setup:**
Open the shared FigJam board (or your own Notion template). You'll see a 5-column table: Empathise · Define · Ideate · Prototype · Test.

**Instructions:**

1. **(2 min)** Write your project name at the top. In 1–2 sentences, describe what it is and who the users are.

2. **(6 min)** For each stage, fill in:
   - **Activity** — What will you do? (e.g. user interviews, HMW workshop, wireframes)
   - **Output** — What will you produce? (e.g. empathy map, problem statement, prototype)

   Don't try to fill every cell — even 1–2 per stage is a real plan.

3. **(2 min)** Share back: one stage where you're confident about the activity, and one stage where you're not sure what to do.

> **AI in Practice:** Paste this prompt into your AI tool:
> *"I'm a UX designer working on [project description]. My users are [who they are]. Generate a Design Process overview for each Design Thinking stage. For each stage, suggest: 1 key activity, 1 output to produce, and 1 sign that it's safe to move to the next stage. Also flag the biggest risk if I skip each stage."*
>
> After seeing the output, discuss: What did the AI suggest that you wouldn't have thought of? What did it miss about your specific project context?

## AI in Practice

### 🤖 Try this prompt

> *"I'm a UX designer starting a project: [describe your project in 1–2 sentences]. My users are [who they are]. Help me map out a Design Thinking process for this project. For each stage (Empathise, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test), suggest: 1 key activity, 1 output to produce, and 1 sign that I'm ready to move to the next stage."*

### 🧠 Critical thinking

After seeing the AI output — which suggested activities feel realistic for your team's time and access to users? Did the AI make assumptions about your project that aren't true? Which stage does the AI treat most superficially?

### ✍️ Prompt engineering tip

The more context you give, the more useful the output. Instead of "a banking app", try "a home loan application tool used by mortgage brokers at a major Australian bank, on desktop, with 20–30 complex form screens." Specificity forces the AI to give you relevant activities rather than generic Design Thinking theory.

## Assignments

### Assignment 1 — Set Up Your Design Process
**Due:** Before next session
**Time estimate:** 45–60 min
**Type:** Planning · Project management tool

Choose a real project. Set up the 5-stage Design Thinking process as a task list or board in your project management tool (Jira, Notion, Planner, or equivalent).

1. Choose your real project
2. For each Design Thinking stage, add at least 1 activity and 1 output as tasks
3. Assign rough timing to each stage
4. Share your board with your team and agree on a shared workspace

**Deliverable:** Link to your live project board. Be ready to walk through it in the next session.

> **AI in Practice:** *"I'm a UX designer starting a project: [describe your project in 1–2 sentences]. My users are [who they are]. For each Design Thinking stage, suggest: 1 key activity, 1 output to produce, and 1 sign that I'm ready to move to the next stage."*

---

### Assignment 2 — Complete the Design Brief
**Due:** Before next session
**Time estimate:** 30–45 min
**Type:** Documentation · Notion

Open your Design Brief in Notion and fill in what you know right now — don't wait to have all the answers.

1. Write a first draft of your Problem Statement
2. Describe your primary user in 2–3 sentences
3. List 3 design goals
4. Note any constraints you already know about
5. After your stakeholder interview (Assignment 4) — come back and update it

**Deliverable:** Notion link shared with your mentor. Be ready to discuss in the next session.

> **AI in Practice:** *"I'm a UX designer working on [project]. Help me write 3 versions of a Problem Statement using this structure: [User] needs a way to [do something] because [pain point], which results in [negative outcome]. Write one broad, one specific, one from a business perspective. Then tell me which is strongest and why."*

---

### Assignment 3 — Capture Current Screens
**Due:** Before next session
**Time estimate:** 30 min
**Type:** Documentation · FigJam or Figma

For each main experience in your project, capture the current screen flow as it exists today.

1. Identify the key experiences to map (e.g. Onboarding, Core Task, Checkout, Settings)
2. Screenshot each screen in the flow — aim for a high-level overview, not every micro-state
3. Arrange in a screen flow layout — label each screen with its state
4. We will use these flows in the next session to set up friction metrics for each step

**Deliverable:** Figma or FigJam link with your current screen flows. Come ready to discuss friction points.

---

### Assignment 4 — Run a Stakeholder Interview
**Due:** Before next session
**Time estimate:** 60–90 min
**Type:** Research · Real conversation

Run 1 stakeholder interview with someone connected to your project (client, subject-matter expert, product owner, or engineering lead).

1. Prepare a script with 5–8 questions — focus on: current user experience, pain points, constraints, and what success looks like
2. Ask for permission to record (optional but useful)
3. Take notes during or immediately after
4. Share back key findings
5. Update your design brief based on what you learned

**Deliverable:** Written notes or summary shared with your mentor before next session.

> **AI in Practice:** *"I'm a UX designer working on [project description]. My users are [who they are]. Key assumptions from my brief audit: [paste list]. Generate a stakeholder interview script. For each question, tell me: what assumption it tests, and what a strong vs. weak answer would look like."*
