01Part One

Affinity
Mapping

A method for clustering raw research observations into themes — so patterns emerge instead of staying buried in your notes.

Part 01 · Affinity Mapping

What is Affinity Mapping?

Cluster raw research observations into themes so patterns emerge instead of staying buried in your notes.

One observation → One sticky
Cluster by feeling, not logic
Name the cluster → That's your theme
01

One note, one observation

Each quote, behaviour, or data point lives on its own sticky. No grouping yet.

02

Move notes that feel related

Group silently first — don't discuss, just feel. Let the clusters find themselves.

03

Name the cluster

Write a header that captures what unites that group. That header is your theme.

Part 01 · Affinity Mapping

How to Run It — 5 Steps

01 · Dump

Everything out

  • One observation per sticky
  • FigJam, Miro, or physical
  • No filtering yet
02 · Absorb

Read each note

  • Don't sort yet
  • Absorb the full weight
  • Feel the data as a whole
03 · Move

Start clustering

  • Group by feeling, not logic
  • No labels first
  • Let patterns surface naturally
04 · Name

Label each cluster

  • "What unites these notes?"
  • That header is your theme
  • Keep it short and active
05 · Reflect

Stand back

  • Look for super-themes
  • Spot the outliers
  • Note the surprises
🤖 AI in Practice · Affinity Mapping

Try This Prompt

🤖
Claude Prompt — Affinity Mapping
Here are my raw interview notes: [paste your notes] Cluster these into themes. Give each theme a short name and explain what connects the notes in it.

⚠ Watch out for

AI groups by keywords, not meaning. It may lump a usability problem and an emotional reaction into the same cluster. Always split and rename clusters yourself.

Find the real "so what" — transform observations into implications that matter for your design decisions.

Part 02 · Insight Statements

Insight = Observation + Implication

Observation

What you saw or heard

A direct quote, a behaviour, a pattern from the research. The raw, uninterpreted signal from the field.

"Users kept abandoning the checkout flow after the payment step — even when the transaction went through."
Implication

The "so what"

Why this matters for your design decisions. The meaning behind the observation. This is where insight lives.

"This means the app fails to confirm the transaction clearly — users don't know it worked, which breaks trust at the most critical moment."
Part 02 · Insight Statements

Strong vs Weak Insights

Weak
  • "Users find the app confusing"
  • "Users want a simpler interface"
  • "Users liked the new feature"
Strong
  • "Users can't locate booking status after submission, which causes them to call support — revealing a gap in post-action feedback design"
  • "Hospitality managers visit the app 3–5× per shift but must navigate 4 screens each time — suggesting a need for a persistent dashboard view"
  • "3 of 4 users completed the booking flow faster, but all 4 got stuck at the date picker — a specific interaction failure, not a layout issue"

AI often writes generic implications. If the implication could apply to any product, it's not specific enough — push it further.

03Part Three

How
Might We

Turn an insight into a design opportunity — without jumping straight to a solution.

Part 03 · How Might We

The Three Words That Open Possibility

How

Assumes it's solvable

Not "can we" or "should we" — it's already a given that it can be done. We're past the question of whether.

Might

Leaves the solution open

"Might" invites exploration. No single right answer yet. Multiple solutions can coexist.

We

It's a team challenge

Not one person's burden to solve alone. "We" builds shared ownership before a single line is designed.

Part 03 · How Might We

From Insight to HMW

Insight "Users abandon after checkout even when the transaction succeeds — because there is no clear confirmation — breaking trust at the most critical moment in the flow."

HMW 01

…make it immediately clear that a transaction was successful, without requiring the user to check?

HMW 02

…reduce user anxiety at the moment between action and confirmation?

HMW 03

…design a post-payment experience that builds trust instead of doubt?

AI HMWs can sound polished but be too vague to act on. Check: can you actually design something to answer each one?

04Part Four

Problem
Statement

Land on what to solve — one clear, user-centred problem the whole team can align on.

Part 04 · Problem Statement

What Makes a Strong Problem Statement?

01

User-centred

It describes a real person's problem — not a business goal, not a feature request, not a technical constraint.

02

Specific, not vague

"Users struggle to track booking status" beats "users find the product confusing." Precision makes it testable.

03

Open to solutions

A good problem statement doesn't hint at the answer. It opens possibility instead of closing it down.

Part 04 · Problem Statement — The Formula

[User type] needs a way to [goal]
because [insight / barrier]

"First-time online shoppers need a way to know their order was confirmed immediately after payment because the current delay and lack of feedback causes them to abandon — and sometimes re-order — thinking the transaction failed."

Part 04 · Problem Statement

Strong vs Weak Problem Statements

Weak Why weak Strong
"We need to redesign the checkout flow" Solution, not a problem "First-time buyers need a way to confirm their order was placed because the lack of feedback after payment causes them to re-submit, creating duplicate orders"
"Users don't trust the app" Too vague to act on "New users need a way to verify their account is set up correctly because there is no onboarding confirmation — leading them to contact support before their first action"
"The app needs better notifications" Solution, not user-centred "Frequent users need a way to know when a status change happens without opening the app because they currently miss time-sensitive updates — causing delays and frustration"
🤖 AI in Practice · Problem Statement

Try This Prompt

🤖
Claude Prompt — Problem Statement
Here is my research finding: [paste insight or HMW] Help me write a problem statement using this formula: [User] needs a way to [goal] because [barrier].

⚠ Watch out for

AI often fills the "because" with logical-sounding assumptions. Make sure the barrier comes from what users actually said — not what seems reasonable.

↩ Reflect

Would a designer who wasn't in your sessions understand exactly who you're designing for from this statement alone?
