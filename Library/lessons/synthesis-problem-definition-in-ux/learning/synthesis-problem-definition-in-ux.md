# Synthesis & Problem Definition in UX

**Type:** Lesson
**Duration:** 90–120 minutes
**Audience:** Intermediate
**Methods:** Affinity Mapping · Insight Statements · How Might We (HMW) · Problem Statement

---

## Key Concepts

### Synthesis
The process of moving raw data up the ladder of abstraction — turning observations into insights, and insights into a problem worth solving. Most research stops at observation. Synthesis is where direction begins.

### The Pipeline
The four-step sequence that connects raw data to a design direction: Affinity Mapping → Insight Statements → How Might We → Problem Statement. Each step has a clear input and output, and each feeds the next. You can't skip steps without losing fidelity.

### Workshop Synthesis
Running synthesis as a facilitated team session — rather than solo analysis followed by a report — builds shared understanding and stakeholder alignment as a byproduct of the work itself. The designer acts as facilitator, not expert. Buy-in is baked in because meaning is built together, not delivered to a passive audience.

### Affinity Mapping
The first move in the pipeline. A method for clustering raw research observations into themes — so patterns emerge instead of staying buried in notes. One observation per sticky, silent clustering first, label after.

### Insight Statements
The second move. Insight = Observation + Implication. An observation tells you what happened; an insight tells you what it means for design. The most common mistake is stopping at the observation and calling it insight.

### How Might We (HMW)
The third move. A framing technique that turns an insight into a design opportunity without jumping to a solution. A good HMW hits the Goldilocks zone: specific enough to act on, open enough to be answered in five completely different ways.

### Problem Statement
The output of the pipeline. One clear, user-centred statement that the whole team can align on. Uses the formula: [User] needs a way to [goal] because [barrier].

## Practice Activities

### Affinity Mapping Practice
Groups of 2–3 work through a shared dataset of research findings. Each finding goes on a separate sticky, groups cluster silently, then label themes and write one insight statement per cluster. Ends with a cross-group share-back.

### HMW Writing
Each student takes their strongest insight from the affinity mapping exercise and writes three HMW statements ranging from too narrow to too broad. Pairs pick the one that hits the Goldilocks zone and explain their reasoning.
