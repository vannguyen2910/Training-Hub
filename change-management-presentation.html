The Problem

Why Change Management Matters

>60%
of change projects fail to fully achieve their goals
  • ●Increasing speed of organizational change
  • ●Growing pressure to adapt to market forces
  • ●Need for a structured, repeatable approach
  • ●Without it: wasted resources, resistance, failure
Taxonomy

Three Levels of Change

① Change
Small – Medium Scale
Department-specific projects with contained impact.

Example: Analytics tool adoption
② Conversion
Cross-Departmental
Visible effects across departments, but no fundamental strategic rethink.

Example: Customer service workflow redesign
③ Transformation
Full Strategic Realignment
Entire organization affected. Shift in business fields or identity.

Example: Preussag → TUI (steel to tourism)
Definition

What is Change Management?

"Preparation, analysis, planning, implementation, evaluation and continuous further development of integrated change measures."
— Vahs & Weiand
Rank & Scheinpflug Add:
A permanent, planned procedure — not a one-time event. Requires two dimensions:
Effectiveness
Achieving goals + employee acceptance
Efficiency
Time and cost optimization
Typology

Two Forms of Change

1st Order
Evolutionary (Gradual)
  • ●Limited scope
  • ●No fundamental redesign
  • ●Less fear & resistance
  • ●Easier to implement
2nd Order
Revolutionary (Radical)
  • ●Affects entire organization
  • ●Breaks with the past
  • ●High uncertainty & resistance
  • ●Requires strong leadership
Depth Framework

Vahs & Weiand's Layer Model

Four Depth Levels of Change

Level Type Description Effort
1 Restructuring Optimize existing processes & structures Low
2 Reorientation Strategic realignment, redesign business fields Medium
3 Revitalisation Change competencies & employee behavior High
4 Remodeling Change core values & organizational culture Very High

Psychology of Change

Lewin's Three-Phase Model

1
Unfreezing
Generate willingness to change. Question old practices. Create awareness of need.

Cannot be skipped.
→
2
Moving
Develop new behaviors and thinking. Work with new approach. Most uncertain phase — requires ongoing support & training.
→
3
Freezing
Anchor new behaviors permanently. Without this: old habits return. Reinforce through feedback & monitoring.
All three phases must be navigated — skipping any phase undermines the entire change effort.

Evidence

Success Rates — Reality Check

StudyKey Finding
Capgemini (2010) 66% avg achievement; only 1 in 8 fully succeeded; 1 in 4 was a flop
IBM (2007) <40% met targets fully; 16% failed entirely
C4 Consulting (2006) <20% complete success; >1 in 3 deemed failed or barely successful
Why the gap?
  • ●Lack of management commitment
  • ●Unclear goals and vision
  • ●Poor communication
  • ●Failure to involve employees
  • ●Unrealistic planning
Integrated Approach (Vahs & Weiand)

Four Fields of Action

🎯
Strategy
Develop competitive advantages — reactive or proactive to market shifts
🌱
Corporate Culture
Values, standards, attitudes that anchor change in lasting behavior
⚙️
Technology
Production methods, tools, and IT systems enabling the change
🏗️
Organisation
Processes, structures, cost efficiency, and throughput optimization
Key insight: Changes in one field ripple to all others — all four must be coordinated.
How to Succeed

Critical Success Factors

  • ✓
    Clear Leadership & Commitment — Top management must visibly champion the change
  • ✓
    Transparent Communication — Explain the "why," share expected outcomes, give regular updates
  • ✓
    Employee Involvement — Involve those affected early; address concerns and resistance
  • ✓
    Realistic Planning — Set achievable milestones; allow adequate time for Unfreezing & Freezing
  • ✓
    Process-Oriented Goals — Monitor how the change is managed, not just the outcomes
Summary

Key Takeaways

  • ●Change is inevitable but challenging — over 60% of projects struggle to deliver
  • ●A systematic approach is required: definition → planning → implementation → stabilization
  • ●Psychological dimension is critical — Lewin's three phases cannot be skipped
  • ●All four dimensions must align: strategy, culture, technology, and organization
  • ●Success requires clear goals, committed leadership, employee buy-in, and realistic timelines
  • ●Change management is ongoing — not a one-time project, but a continuous capability
