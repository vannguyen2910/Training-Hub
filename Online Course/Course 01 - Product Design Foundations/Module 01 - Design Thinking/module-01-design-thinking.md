## Module Overview

Most designers don't struggle because they lack design skills. They struggle because they skip the thinking that makes design decisions defensible.

This module introduces Design Thinking — not as abstract methodology, but as a practical process that changes how you start, run, and close design work. You'll learn why most design teams fail before they open Figma, how the 5-stage framework actually works in practice, and how to apply it immediately to your own real project.

**By the end of this module, you'll be able to:**
1. Explain what Design Thinking is in language you could use with a product manager or stakeholder
2. Identify the 5 stages of the Design Thinking cycle and what question each one is trying to answer
3. Recognise where your own design process typically breaks down — and why that stage matters
4. Draft a project-specific design process map that names the activities and outputs for each stage

**What's included:**
- 7 short videos (~10 min each)
- 1 downloadable FigJam/Miro template: Design Process Map
- 4 assignments to apply the framework to your real project
- AI prompts for each stage

## Video Breakdown

### Video 1 — Why Designers Keep Failing (10 min)
*The real problem isn't your skills — it's your process.*

Most designers spend 90% of their time in tools, visual design, and UX craft. These matter. But the things that actually drive career growth — design process, communication, collaboration, ownership — rarely get developed.

The pattern that kills most design work looks like this: a requirement arrives → you open Figma → you produce high-fidelity screens. Nobody questioned the brief. Nobody validated whether the problem was real. Six months later, the product ships and users don't respond the way anyone expected.

This is not a skills problem. It's a process problem — specifically, the absence of a structured approach for understanding the problem before designing the solution.

**The difference a process makes:**

| Without a design process | With a design process |
|---|---|
| Solutions decided before problems are understood | Decisions grounded in user evidence |
| Briefs treated as truth | Assumptions surfaced early |
| Assumptions invisible until it's too late | Research actually changes what gets built |
| Late pivots are expensive | Fewer surprises at launch |

### Video 2 — What Design Thinking Actually Is (10 min)
*Human-centered. Problem-first. Not a checklist.*

Design Thinking is a human-centered problem-solving method that helps teams understand user needs, challenge assumptions, and create innovative solutions before designing anything.

The key word is *human-centered*. It doesn't start with a business requirement or a technical constraint. It starts with understanding the people the product is for — their context, their actual behaviour, their unspoken needs — before any solution is proposed.

**The Product Designer Capability Pyramid**

The Artiom Dashinsky pyramid describes what product designers are actually evaluated on:

- **Base:** Tools, Visual Design, UX Design — the skills most hiring tests focus on
- **Middle:** Collaboration, Communication, Design Process — what determines day-to-day effectiveness
- **Top:** Ownership, Strategy, Mentorship, Advocacy — what drives career growth and business impact

Design Thinking is the methodological foundation for the Design Process layer. It doesn't replace craft. It's the decision-making structure that makes craft decisions worth making.

### Video 3 — The 5-Stage Cycle (10 min)
*The framework most teams skip the most important parts of.*

**01 — EMPATHISE** · *What is actually going on with users — not what we assume?*
Understand the people you're designing for: their feelings, motivations, behaviours, and pain points. Done before any solution thinking begins.

**02 — DEFINE** · *What is the real problem worth solving?*
Using what you learned in Empathise, articulate a clear, specific problem statement that your team can design against. A brief is not a problem definition.

**03 — IDEATE** · *What are all the possible ways to solve it?*
Quantity before quality. Generate as many ideas as possible before evaluating them. Most teams start here — which means they're solving a problem they've never validated.

**04 — PROTOTYPE** · *What is the fastest way to make this testable?*
Build the minimum thing that allows you to test your ideas. Not a finished product — an argument you can test.

**05 — TEST** · *Were we right? What do we need to change?*
Show your prototype to real users and observe what happens. Synthesise findings. Return to earlier stages if the learning demands it.

> **Important:** Design Thinking is non-linear. You move freely between stages as insights emerge. The stages are a scaffold, not a fixed sequence.

**The pattern most teams follow in reality:**
Most teams skip stages 1 and 2 entirely — not because they don't know them, but because nobody asked for them. The brief arrives from a stakeholder, treated as if it already contains the problem definition. The team moves straight to Ideate.

*"A brief is not a problem definition."*

### Video 4 — Stage 1: Empathise in Depth (10 min)
*Discovery and synthesis before any solution thinking.*

The Empathise stage is about deeply understanding the people you're designing for. There are two types of activities: discovery (understand users) and synthesis (turn observations into a clear picture).

**Discovery activities:**
- **Desk research** — competitor review, market reports, analytics, existing research
- **User survey** — quantitative snapshot; use to supplement interviews, not replace them
- **User interviews** — the richest source of qualitative insight
- **Shadowing / observation** — surface workarounds and habits users don't mention
- **Stakeholder interviews** — understand business context and constraints
- **Support ticket / NPS analysis** — mine what users are already saying

**Synthesis activities:**
- **Empathy mapping** — what users say, think, do, and feel (SAY / THINK / DO / FEEL)
- **Assumption Map** — surface everything the team believes without evidence
- **Research Log** — add an entry after every user conversation: quote, behaviour, context

---

### Video 5 — Stage 2: Define in Depth (10 min)
*Turning raw research into a problem worth solving.*

**Key tools:**
- **Lean UX Canvas** — frames the business problem, user, solution hypothesis, metrics, and assumptions in one view
- **Jobs-to-Be-Done (JTBD)** — identifies functional, emotional, and social jobs. Most designs miss the emotional and social jobs, which is often where the real value lives
- **How Might We (HMW)** — converts each insight into a design opportunity question. Not too broad (unsolvable) and not too narrow (already a solution in disguise)
- **Current-state journey mapping** — maps the experience as it actually is, not as it should be

---

### Video 6 — Stages 3, 4, 5: Ideate, Prototype, Test (10 min)
*A practical overview of the stages you have more intuitive access to.*

**Ideate — generate activities:**
- Crazy 8s: 8 sketches in 8 minutes. Forces volume, kills perfectionism
- HMW ideation: take each HMW question and generate 5–10 ideas against it
- Brainwriting: silent parallel idea generation — prevents loudest-voice bias

**Ideate — select activities:**
- Impact vs effort matrix: focus on high-impact, low-effort ideas
- Concept sketching: develop top 2–3 directions into fuller sketches

**Prototype:**
- Wireframes: structural layout, no colour or brand distraction
- Interactive prototype: states, transitions, navigation demos
- POC: answers one question — does this idea actually work?

**Test:**
- Concept testing: show a concept to 3 users, measure comprehension
- Moderated usability testing: observe a user completing real tasks with think-aloud protocol. 5 users reveals ~85% of critical issues
- A/B testing: compare two live versions — answers "which is better?" not "why?"

## Exercise — Draft Your Design Process Map

**Download:** [Design Process Map Template — FigJam] *(link to your template)*

**What you're doing:** Applying the 5-stage Design Thinking framework to your own real project.

**Instructions:**

1. Open the template. You'll see a 5-column table: Empathise · Define · Ideate · Prototype · Test.

2. Write your project name at the top. In 1–2 sentences, describe what it is and who the users are.

3. For each stage, fill in:
   - **Activity** — what will you do? (e.g. user interviews, HMW workshop, wireframes)
   - **Output** — what will you produce? (e.g. empathy map, problem statement, prototype)

   Don't try to fill every cell — even 1–2 per stage is a real plan.

4. Note one stage where you're confident about the activity, and one where you're not sure what to do. You'll revisit this in the community forum.

**AI prompt to try:**
> *"I'm a UX designer working on [project description]. My users are [who they are]. Generate a Design Process overview for each Design Thinking stage. For each stage, suggest: 1 key activity, 1 output to produce, and 1 sign that it's safe to move to the next stage. Also flag the biggest risk if I skip each stage."*

After you see the output: which activities feel realistic for your situation? What did the AI miss about your specific project context?

## Assignments

Complete these before starting Module 2. They build directly into the next module's exercises.

### Assignment 1 — Set Up Your Design Process Board
**Time estimate:** 45–60 min

Choose your real project. Set up the 5-stage Design Thinking process as a task list or board in your project management tool (Notion, Jira, Planner, or equivalent).

1. For each Design Thinking stage, add at least 1 activity and 1 output as tasks
2. Assign rough timing to each stage
3. Share the board link in the community forum — you'll reference it throughout the course

**AI prompt:**
> *"I'm a UX designer starting a project: [describe in 1–2 sentences]. My users are [who they are]. For each Design Thinking stage, suggest: 1 key activity, 1 output to produce, and 1 sign that I'm ready to move to the next stage."*

---

### Assignment 2 — Complete Your Design Brief
**Time estimate:** 30–45 min

Open a new doc (Notion, Google Docs — wherever you work) and write a first draft of your Design Brief. Don't wait to have all the answers.

1. Write a first draft of your Problem Statement
2. Describe your primary user in 2–3 sentences
3. List 3 design goals
4. Note any constraints you already know about

**AI prompt:**
> *"I'm a UX designer working on [project]. Help me write 3 versions of a Problem Statement using this structure: [User] needs a way to [do something] because [pain point], which results in [negative outcome]. Write one broad, one specific, one from a business perspective. Then tell me which is strongest and why."*

---

### Assignment 3 — Capture Your Current Screen Flows
**Time estimate:** 30 min

For each main experience in your project, capture the current screen flow as it exists today.

1. Identify the key experiences to map (e.g. onboarding, core task, checkout)
2. Screenshot each screen in the flow
3. Arrange them in a flow layout in FigJam or Figma — label each screen with its state

You'll use these screen flows in Module 2 to run a friction analysis.

---

### Assignment 4 — Run a Stakeholder Interview
**Time estimate:** 60–90 min

Run 1 stakeholder interview with someone connected to your project (client, subject-matter expert, product owner, or engineering lead).

1. Prepare a script with 5–8 questions — focus on: current user experience, pain points, constraints, what success looks like
2. Take notes during or immediately after
3. Update your Design Brief based on what you learned

**AI prompt:**
> *"I'm a UX designer working on [project description]. My users are [who they are]. Key assumptions from my brief audit: [paste list]. Generate a stakeholder interview script. For each question, tell me: what assumption it tests, and what a strong vs weak answer looks like."*
